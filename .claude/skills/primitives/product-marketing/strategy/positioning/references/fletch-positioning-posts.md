### Business Outcomes are unspecific

I reviewed hundreds of homepages last week for a post.
The number one culprit that made them unclear?

👇🏻
Leading with business outcomes

Homepage after homepage simply stated what high level business outcome the company promised to influence:

"Increase your revenue"
"Reduce your costs"
"Get more customers"

The issue is that business outcomes are the function of many different things.

And any number of companies could claim to drive the same outcome.

You also aren't doing yourself any favors when it comes to differentiating from the competition (because they all claim to drive those same identical business outcomes.)

So by simply stating a business outcome, you haven't actually told your customers anything about what your product actually is.

If you never say what your product is...

There's one positioning decision that is more important than any other
👇🏻

Are you going to position within an existing category or not?

Positioning is helping people understand your value relative to the alternatives.

It's the art of choosing what space you want to occupy in a prospect's mind.

Their minds are already filled with lots of shelves (i.e. existing product categories).

An easy way to gain space is to fight for a spot on one of those shelves.

But this strategy has its drawbacks (as you can see in the carousel below).

Another strategy is to find white space in a prospect's brain and work to become the go-to tool for a task or initiative they are trying to accomplish.

These tasks/initaitives/workflows ALSO have permanent space in a prospect's mind, so linking yourself to one of them is ALSO a surefire way to gain a defined space in their minds.

But this strategy (like the other) has its own drawbacks.

### You’re building your ICP wrong

Here's how to build your ICP the right way
👇🏻 👇🏻 👇🏻

We've worked with 500 companies, and they all describe their ideal customer profiles the same way:

→ they list a bunch of firmographic details

You can't blame them, because this is how EVERYONE thinks of segments.

But B2C learned a principle that B2B hasn't quite embraced:
→ using persona-based segments kinda sucks

Just because someone is a soccer mom that lives in Utah with a high income doesn't mean she will buy a Range Rover.

People shop for products not primarily based on personal descriptors... but based on specific needs (or "jobs-to-be-done" if you're a follower of that methodology).

This is the same with B2B.

Companies don't shop for things based on their headcount, industry, size, etc.

They shop for specific things based on their actual workflows.

These can be considered "use case-based segments" or "category-based segments."

A better place to start when trying to carve out an ICP is with the actual workflows your product supports OR the category you fall into that a company would either have or would be shopping for.

### Why your startup can’t agree on a single message

Many marketing leaders are ignorant...and they don’t even realize it.

Ignorant of what a market actually is.

Ignorant in what it takes to communicate with a market.

As they’ve climbed the ladder working on “bigger” projects for “bigger” companies, they’ve convinced themselves that their messaging also must be “bigger”.

And “bigger” messaging means broader summarizations.

This is a big mistake. (see what I did there?)

These leaders have forgotten the first principles of B2B markets.

B2B markets are made of companies.

Companies come in a lot of different flavors (business models, industries, missions, etc.)

Companies are made of people, who also come in a lot of different flavors.

People are complex creatures.

They hold different beliefs.

They take different approaches to problems.

The tools they use (your competition) are unique.

❌ So the idea that you can communicate across all this fragmentation and uniqueness with a single big message is downright comical — it's not specific enough to resonate with anyone.

### Why sales is awesome (And sucks) at positioning

CMOs: “We need one clear story.”

VP of Sales: “Cool, I’ve got 17 of them.”

👆This is why positioning decisions rarely get made when both these people are in the room

(and also why sales and marketing secretly hate each other)

We see it all the time in our positioning work with B2B startups.

Here is how it goes:

👉 Sales leaders are actually mini geniuses when it comes to “creating” positioning strategies.

(maybe we shouldn't tell them this, it's going to go right to their heads)

They can frame a customer’s problem on the fly and craft arguments that make the product irresistible.

But this is where it falls apart:

👎 They never stick to one story.

Instead, they:..

…dodge…

…dip…

…dive…

…duck…

…and dodge again.

Reshaping the pitch for every single prospect.

That’s sales. And it’s valuable.

But it’s not marketing.

Marketing doesn’t get a back-and-forth.

(ie. they cant' dip and dodge)

It’s one way messaging — and it requires a single compelling narrative thread.

This divergence is where the CMO vs. VP of Sales tension lives.
