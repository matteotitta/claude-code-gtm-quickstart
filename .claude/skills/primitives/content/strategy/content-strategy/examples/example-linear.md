# Content Strategy Example: Linear

**Company:** Linear
**Category:** Issue tracking + project management for software teams
**Strategy period:** Inferred from public footprint, captured 2026-05

---

## Context

This example demonstrates content strategy for a B2B SaaS company that competes on **opinionated craft** — a saturated category (Atlassian Jira, Asana, Notion, Monday) where the differentiator isn't features but design taste, speed, and developer-grade UX. Linear's content engine doubles down on that positioning at every touch.

All facts below are derived from publicly observable artifacts: linear.app, linear.app/blog, linear.app/method, linear.app/changelog, founder posts on X/LinkedIn, the Method (their public ops handbook), and the public Linear Slack community. No internal information.

---

## 1. Strategy Foundations

### Content goals

| Goal | Category | Public success signal | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand-led pipeline (zero outbound, content drives signups) | Revenue growth | Self-serve signup velocity reflected in published growth posts | 1 |
| Category re-positioning (define "the issue tracker for craft-driven teams") | Company awareness | "Linear" cited as the design benchmark in dev-tooling discourse | 2 |
| Talent magnet (small team, hire-from-inbox) | Employer branding | Career page traffic, public job posts oversubscribed | 3 |
| Method authority (Linear Method as a referenced ops doctrine) | Thought leadership | External writers citing the Method as a planning standard | 4 |

[VERIFIED: linear.app/method, linear.app/blog, accessed 2026-05-05]

### Audience

Single-persona discipline. Linear writes to **engineering team leads at fast-moving SaaS startups** — typically 5-50 engineers, not yet committed to Jira, design-quality-conscious. Every piece of content gets evaluated against "would the typical Linear customer share this?"

Secondary audience: design-conscious operators (CTO, head of product) who influence the buying decision.

### Voice

- Quiet confidence, no superlatives, no exclamation marks.
- Sentence-case headlines.
- Em dashes used sparingly with spaces.
- No corporate buzzwords. The blog post about a feature reads like a colleague describing a workflow, not a marketer hitting points.
- Long-form is rare; when it ships, it's substantive (Method docs, retrospective posts).

## 2. Channels + cadence

| Channel | Format | Cadence | What earns the spot |
|---|---|---|---|
| Changelog (linear.app/changelog) | Visual feature releases with looping mp4 demos | Weekly | Ship cadence is the content. Every release is the post. |
| Blog (linear.app/blog) | Long-form ops + craft posts | ~2/month | Method updates, customer stories, founder essays, post-mortems |
| Linear Method (linear.app/method) | Living ops handbook | Quarterly updates | Public ops standard — used by other teams as reference |
| X / Twitter | Short-form behind-the-scenes + product demos | Daily-ish | Founders + designers post directly; small team voice |
| Customer stories | Case-study format with metrics | Monthly | Public-facing customer logos + workflow diagrams |
| Press / podcasts | Founder interviews | Opportunistic | Karri (CEO) on selected pods; not a volume play |

The discipline is publishing **less, better**. Cadence is steady but never high — every artifact is over-invested.

---

## 3. Pillar topics

Three reusable pillars that every output traces back to:

1. **Craft + opinionated tooling** — why software teams should care about the tool's UX, not just its feature checklist. Hero pillar; ~50% of long-form.
2. **The Linear Method** — public planning + project management doctrine. Method handbook, retrospectives, ops experiments. ~30%.
3. **Customer workflows** — how specific software teams use Linear to ship faster. Real customer logos (Cash App, Vercel, Ramp, Loom). ~20%.

Notably absent: generic "5 tips for project management," competitor takedowns, paid SEO content, AI-thought-leadership-of-the-week. Linear doesn't fight that battle.

---

## 4. Editorial calendar pattern

Quarterly rhythm:

- **Month 1 of quarter:** Method update (one major doctrine refinement) + 1-2 customer stories
- **Month 2:** Founder essay or post-mortem (long-form craft piece)
- **Month 3:** Quarterly product retrospective + community spotlight

Weekly rhythm overlaid:

- **Tuesday:** Changelog post
- **Thursday:** Blog post (when one is in flight)
- **Continuous:** X posts from founders + designers as ship moments happen

## 5. What this example demonstrates for your own strategy

**If your category is saturated, compete on a non-feature axis.** Linear chose craft + speed as their axis; the entire content engine reinforces it. Your axis might be transparency (Buffer), opinionated workflow (Basecamp), or developer-first ergonomics (Vercel) — the lesson is pick one and don't dilute.

**Ship cadence IS the content stream.** If you're shipping weekly, your changelog earns audience without a separate content engine. Don't build a blog if your product is the blog.

**Less, better.** Linear's blog has under 100 posts in 6 years. Each is over-invested. The opposite of "publish 4x a week and pray."

**Customer stories with logos > unnamed case studies.** Linear's public customer list (Cash App, Ramp, Vercel, Loom) is itself a positioning artifact. If you can earn named logos, that beats every "B2B SaaS startup" anonymized case study.

**Method authority compounds.** The Linear Method is now cited in third-party ops content as a standard. Building a public methodology — and updating it publicly — creates a flywheel content marketing alone can't.

---

## 6. What this example does NOT show

- Paid acquisition strategy (Linear publicly says they don't do paid; not all companies have that luxury)
- Outbound sales motion (PLG-first; this strategy assumes self-serve signup is the conversion mechanic)
- Localized / multi-language content (single language)
- Community management at scale (Linear's community is small + curated, not a Discord-style growth play)

Use this as a model for craft-led B2B SaaS in a mature category. For category-creation strategies (where the buyer doesn't yet know the category exists), the playbook is heavier on educational content + role definition — different shape entirely.
