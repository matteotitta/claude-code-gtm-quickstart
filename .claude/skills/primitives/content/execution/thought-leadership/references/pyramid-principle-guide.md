# Barbara Minto Pyramid Principle — Deep Dive

## Origins

Barbara Minto developed the Pyramid Principle at McKinsey in the 1960s. It remains the gold standard for structuring persuasive arguments in consulting, business writing, and thought leadership.

## Core Concept

**Lead with the answer, then prove it.**

Most people write inductively — building up to a conclusion. The Pyramid Principle flips this: state your conclusion first, then support it with grouped, summarized arguments.

```
INDUCTIVE (WRONG FOR BUSINESS)          PYRAMID (RIGHT FOR BUSINESS)
─────────────────────────────           ─────────────────────────────
Evidence 1                              GOVERNING THOUGHT
    ↓                                         ↓
Evidence 2                              ┌─────┼─────┐
    ↓                                   │     │     │
Evidence 3                              KL1  KL2   KL3
    ↓                                   │     │     │
Therefore: Conclusion                   E1   E2    E3
```

## The Three Rules

### Rule 1: Ideas at any level must be summaries of the ideas below

Every level of the pyramid summarizes what's beneath it. The governing thought summarizes the key lines. Each key line summarizes its evidence.

**Test:** Can you remove the lower level and still understand the point? If yes, the summary works.

### Rule 2: Ideas in each grouping must be the same kind of idea

Key line arguments must be parallel in nature:
- All reasons why something is true
- All steps in a process
- All components of a structure
- All comparisons to alternatives

**Bad:** "We should expand because (1) the market is growing, (2) here's how we'd staff it, (3) competitors are weak."
- Mixing market reason, operational detail, and competitive analysis

**Good:** "We should expand because (1) market demand exceeds our capacity, (2) competitor gaps leave customers underserved, (3) our unit economics support scale."
- All three are market/strategic reasons

### Rule 3: Ideas in each grouping must be logically ordered

Logical orders include:
- **Chronological** — First, then, finally
- **Structural** — Geography, organizational units, components
- **Degree** — Most to least important, largest to smallest
- **Categorical** — Groups that are MECE

## MECE: The Structure Test

MECE = Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive

**Mutually Exclusive:** No overlap between items
**Collectively Exhaustive:** No gaps — together they cover everything

### Testing for MECE

Ask:
1. "Is there overlap between any two items?" → If yes, combine or redefine
2. "Is there a major category missing?" → If yes, add it
3. "Do these items together fully support the point above?" → If no, something's missing

### MECE Examples

**MECE:**
- Revenue can grow by: (1) acquiring new customers, (2) increasing average order value, (3) increasing purchase frequency
- Exhaustive: covers all revenue growth levers
- Exclusive: each is distinct

**Not MECE:**
- Revenue can grow by: (1) better marketing, (2) new customer acquisition, (3) expansion into Europe
- Problem: Marketing and acquisition overlap; Europe is a subset of acquisition

## Building the Pyramid: Step by Step

### Step 1: Identify Your Governing Thought

Ask: "If the reader only remembers one thing, what should it be?"

The governing thought must:
- Be a complete sentence (not a topic label)
- Be specific and actionable
- Pass the "so what?" test
- Be slightly uncomfortable to state baldly (if it's obvious, it's not insight)

### Step 2: Identify Your Key Line Arguments

Ask: "What are the 2-4 reasons someone should believe my governing thought?"

Key lines must:
- Directly support the governing thought
- Be MECE
- Each be substantial enough to require evidence
- Each pass the "so what?" test

**Limit to 3-4:** Human working memory holds ~4 items. More than 4 key lines = regroup.

### Step 3: Gather Evidence for Each Key Line

Under each key line, list:
- Facts
- Data points
- Examples
- Anecdotes
- Expert opinions
- Research findings

**Test each piece of evidence:**
- Does it directly support this key line?
- Is it verifiable?
- Is it compelling to the target audience?

### Step 4: Test the Structure

From the top down:
- Does the governing thought summarize the key lines?
- Does each key line summarize its evidence?
- Is everything MECE?

From the bottom up:
- Does the evidence prove the key line?
- Do the key lines prove the governing thought?
- Is there any evidence that doesn't fit?

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Topic Labels Instead of Arguments

**Bad:** "Key Line 1: Market Analysis"
- This is a topic, not an argument

**Good:** "Key Line 1: The market is growing 40% annually and our competitors can't scale."
- This is a complete argument

### Mistake 2: Too Many Key Lines

**Bad:** 7 key line arguments
- Reader can't hold them all

**Good:** Group into 3 categories, then sub-points under each
- Creates a nested pyramid that's still digestible

### Mistake 3: Non-MECE Structure

**Bad:** Key lines that overlap or have gaps

**Fix:** Redraw boundaries until items are truly MECE

### Mistake 4: Evidence That Doesn't Fit

**Bad:** Including an interesting fact that doesn't support any key line

**Fix:** Either it proves a key line (put it there) or it doesn't belong (cut it)

### Mistake 5: Burying the Lead

**Bad:** Building up to the governing thought at the end

**Fix:** State the governing thought first, then prove it

## The "So What?" Test

At every level of the pyramid, ask "so what?" and answer it.

**Governing Thought:** "Most Series A companies get positioning wrong."
So what? → "Which means they waste 6+ months building the wrong messaging."
So what? → "Which means they burn runway and delay product-market fit."

If you can't answer "so what?" with stakes, the idea isn't strong enough.

## Pyramid to Prose: The Translation

| Pyramid Level | Prose Section |
|--------------|---------------|
| Governing thought | Opening paragraph thesis |
| Key line 1 | Section 1 with 2-4 paragraphs |
| Key line 2 | Section 2 with 2-4 paragraphs |
| Key line 3 | Section 3 with 2-4 paragraphs |
| Evidence | Paragraphs within each section |
