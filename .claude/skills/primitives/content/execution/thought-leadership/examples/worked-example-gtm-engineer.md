# Worked Example: "The Rise of the GTM Engineer"

Source: [playbooks.hypergrowthpartners.com/p/the-rise-of-the-gtm-engineer](https://playbooks.hypergrowthpartners.com/p/the-rise-of-the-gtm-engineer)

This example shows how the Pyramid Principle was applied to create a defining thought leadership piece on an emerging role.

---

## Phase 1: Draft (Pyramid Structure)

### Governing Thought

> "The GTM Engineer is an emerging role that combines technical skills with revenue focus — and these people will become the next generation of CROs and CMOs."

**Why this works:**
- Makes a bold, specific claim (emerging role → future executives)
- Defines something new (GTM Engineer)
- Has stakes (career path, organizational structure)
- Passes "so what?" test (readers need to understand this shift)

### Key Line Arguments

**Key Line 1:** "Traditional roles are being productized and compressed by AI and SaaS tools, creating a gap for a new type of operator."

Evidence:
→ ZIRP aftermath forcing "do more with less"
→ GTM SaaS tools productizing SDR, content, analyst work
→ Operationalization requiring data + SaaS stack knowledge
→ Cross-functional demands requiring T-shaped skills

**Key Line 2:** "GTM Engineers are distinct from Growth Marketers, Growth Engineers, Growth PMs, and Growth Hackers — they own pipeline and revenue end-to-end."

Evidence:
→ Comparison table showing what each role owns vs. doesn't own
→ GTM Engineers span acquisition, retention, monetization
→ Technical + strategic + operational hybrid

**Key Line 3:** "Real companies are already seeing results from this approach — 70% conversion rate increases from AI-enabled workflows."

Evidence:
→ Gorgias case study: NLP analysis of migration calls → personalized outreach at scale
→ Datadome: automated lookalike generation triggered by closed-won deals
→ Specific tools mentioned: prompt engineering, multiple AI models

**Key Line 4:** "There's a clear pathway to become a GTM Engineer — either from technical marketing or from engineering backgrounds."

Evidence:
→ Two origin routes mapped
→ Companies actively hiring for this profile (Ramp, Vercel, Ashby, AirOps)
→ Skills checklist: ICP knowledge, customer journey mapping, forecasting, coding

### Potential Objections Addressed

1. "Isn't this just a growth hacker with a new name?"
   → No — growth hackers seek short-term wins; GTM Engineers build sustainable systems

2. "Do you really need technical skills?"
   → Yes — prompt engineering and tool orchestration require technical literacy

3. "Is this role only for big companies?"
   → No — lean teams benefit most from cross-functional operators

### So What? (Implication)

If you're a technical marketer → Build coding skills, this is your career path
If you're hiring → Look for this profile, not siloed specialists
If you're a founder → This is the first growth hire you should make

## Phase 2: Outline (Paragraph Expansion)

### Opening Section

**Hook:** "Inboxes and social feeds are overwhelmed with low-quality automated outreach. Products have never been easier to build — which makes them harder to differentiate."

**Context:** The post-ZIRP world demands efficiency. AI is commoditizing execution. Traditional role boundaries are blurring.

**Thesis:** "A new type of operator is emerging: the GTM Engineer. They combine technical skills with revenue focus, and they're building the next generation of B2B growth."

**Roadmap:** We'll define what makes them different, show real examples, and map the pathway to becoming one.

---

### Section 1: The Case for GTM Engineers

**Lead:** Four forces are driving the emergence of this role.

**Body paragraphs:**
1. ZIRP aftermath — teams must do more with less, specialists are expensive
2. SaaS-ification — tools now do what junior roles used to do
3. Operationalization — growth requires data management and stack orchestration
4. Cross-functionalism — silos don't work in lean teams

**Transition:** So what does this new role actually look like?

---

### Section 2: What Makes GTM Engineers Different

**Lead:** They're not Growth Marketers, Growth Engineers, Growth PMs, or Growth Hackers.

**Body paragraphs:**
1. Comparison: Growth Marketers focus on top-funnel only
2. Comparison: Growth Engineers are confined to product
3. Comparison: Growth PMs manage roadmaps, not execution
4. Comparison: Growth Hackers chase short-term wins

**Key differentiator:** GTM Engineers own pipeline and revenue end-to-end across the full funnel.

**Transition:** But does this work in practice?

---

### Section 3: Real-World Examples

**Lead:** Companies are already seeing results from this approach.

**Body paragraphs:**
1. Gorgias case study — NLP analysis of migration calls, personalized outreach, 70% conversion increase
2. Datadome — automated lookalike generation from closed-won signals
3. Pattern: AI-enabled workflows orchestrated by operators who understand both tech and GTM

**Transition:** How do you become one?

---

### Section 4: The Pathway

**Lead:** Two routes to becoming a GTM Engineer.

**Body paragraphs:**
1. Technical marketers building coding skills
2. Engineers developing revenue/GTM acumen
3. Where to find these roles — growth-focused companies hiring now

---

### Closing Section

**Synthesis:** The GTM Engineer combines what used to be split across multiple roles. They're technical enough to build, strategic enough to prioritize, and revenue-focused enough to matter.

**Implication:** Future CROs and CMOs will come from this background.

**CTA:** If you're building this skillset or hiring for it — this is the playbook.

## Phase 3: Polish (TOV Applied)

### Voice Attributes Applied

☑ **Operator authority:** First-person references, specific tools named (NLP, prompt engineering, CDPs)
☑ **Prescriptive confidence:** "This is the role" — no hedging about whether it's valid
☑ **Framework-driven clarity:** Comparison table, four forces framework, two pathways
☑ **Candid partnership:** Acknowledges objections (not just growth hacker, not just for big companies)
☑ **Specificity over superlatives:** 70% conversion rate, named companies, specific tools
☑ **Conversational warmth:** Accessible language, not academic

### Banned Words Avoided

- No "solutions," "leverage," "synergy"
- No "innovative," "cutting-edge," "game-changer"
- Specific proof points instead of vague claims

### 100 Posts Test

✓ Could only be written by someone who's actually worked on these problems
✓ Takes a position (this role matters, it's distinct, it's the future)
✓ Includes proof from real work (Gorgias, Datadome)
✓ Wouldn't feel generic if published 100 times

---

## Key Learnings from This Example

1. **Start with a bold governing thought** — "GTM Engineers will become the next CROs and CMOs" is a claim worth arguing

2. **Use comparison to define** — Explaining what something isn't is as powerful as explaining what it is

3. **Real examples do heavy lifting** — The Gorgias case study (70% conversion) makes the abstract concrete

4. **Provide a pathway** — Don't just describe the problem, show the reader how to act

5. **Historical context builds credibility** — Framing this as part of a cycle (not a fad) makes it more credible
